# Reflection — What I Learned From Failing Faster

The first version of OpenCal was, by any reasonable measure, a failure. I
shipped it in three weeks, showed it to twenty people, and watched all twenty
of them bounce off the same onboarding screen. My instinct was to fix the
screen. A more experienced engineer on the team suggested we sit with the
failure a little longer before reaching for a solution, and I am grateful I
listened.

What we found when we looked closely was not a design problem. It was an
assumption problem. We had assumed that the people who would use a scheduling
tool were frustrated by the mechanics of scheduling. They were not. They were
frustrated by the social overhead — the back-and-forth, the rescheduling, the
silent obligation to be available. We had built a faster car for people who
wanted to teleport.

The second version of OpenCal was better not because we wrote better code, but
because we asked better questions. That lesson has stayed with me. At Classify
Inc., when I took over operations, the first thing I did was map every step of
the customer onboarding process not from our internal documentation but from
walking alongside a new customer as they went through it. The gap between the
two maps was instructive. There were places where we thought we were being
helpful and were actually introducing friction. There were places where we had
optimised the wrong thing entirely.

I have come to think of that gap — between the map and the territory — as the
most important place to spend time. It is uncomfortable, because it requires
admitting that your mental model is incomplete. But it is also where the most
useful work happens.
