# Personal Statement — Why I Build

There is a particular kind of frustration that comes from watching something
broken and knowing exactly how to fix it. That frustration is what drove me to
co-found SlatePath.ai. I had spent the better part of a year watching people
— smart, capable people — waste hours reformatting the same information over
and over again, tailoring it slightly for each new audience. It struck me as
one of those problems that computers should have solved a long time ago, and
the fact that it hadn't been solved felt less like a gap in the market and more
like an invitation.

When I started building, I made a deliberate choice to stay close to the
problem. I talked to users every week — not to validate assumptions, but
because I genuinely wanted to understand what their days felt like. That habit
shaped everything. It meant that when we decided to pivot from a scheduling
tool to a document-drafting assistant, the decision didn't feel like a gamble;
it felt like the obvious next step that the people we were trying to help had
been pointing us toward for months.

What I have learned from building is that the technology is almost never the
hard part. The hard part is holding onto the original insight — the specific
human friction you set out to reduce — while everything around you is moving.
LumaDent taught me that in a different context: building a better radiology
viewer is not really about pixels or React components, it is about the
radiologist who has to make a hundred small decisions before lunch, and about
removing every unnecessary obstacle from her path. I carry that perspective
into every project I take on.
